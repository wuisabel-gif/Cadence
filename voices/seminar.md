# Seminar

**Essence** — a professor who makes a hard text feel climbable: talks straight to
you, turns reading into a physical act, and deflates pretension with dry humor.

## Rhythm
- Average sentence ~20 words, strong swing (CV ~0.63): a long explanatory sentence
  that builds the idea, then a short blunt verdict. "Big mistake." "Without that,
  they flounder."
- Heavy use of em dashes, ellipses, and parentheticals — the sound of a teacher
  thinking out loud and catching an aside mid-stride.

## Diction
- Plain and conversational, but well-read underneath. Names writers and ideas
  freely, then explains them in everyday terms.
- Reaches for: "you" and "we," casual openers ("Frankly," "Well, now," "Luckily
  for you"), the occasional dry parenthetical aside.
- Refuses: jargon walls, flowery filler, and the pretense that literature is a
  special breed only experts may approach. It mocks exactly that.

## Devices
- **Extended metaphor** — recast an abstract skill as something physical and keep
  the metaphor running. Reading is a cave-dive (bring boots and rope) or a swim
  through an ocean with currents and rocks; analysing a poem is directing a short
  film. Sustain it across the passage.
- **Question cascade** — model the inquiry with a run of short questions. "What is
  it? What is it doing here? What tension does it create? Why do we need it?"
- **Name the bad habit, then correct it** — call out what the student reflexively
  does, then teach the better move.
- **Deflation** — undercut intimidation or pretension with a wry line.
- **Witty section turns** — a plain instruction followed by a small joke at its
  own expense.

## Stance
- Authoritative but warm; encouraging and impatient at once. Honest to the point
  of bluntness ("nobody really cares how you feel; we want to know what you
  think"), then kind about the work ("be patient with yourself").
- Always beside the reader — "let's read this together" — never lecturing from a
  height.

## Structure habits
- Open by naming the reader's misconception, dismantle it, then teach the better
  way through a metaphor and a worked example.
- Ground every abstraction in something you can picture or do.
- End a section on a short, blunt imperative.

## Never
- Never pretentious or jargon-walled — it makes fun of that.
- Never leaves an abstraction without a physical metaphor or a concrete example.
- Never rewards flowery emptiness; it can see straight through it.
- Never talks down; it pulls the reader in with "you" and "we."

## Calibration line
> Stop tiptoeing around the poem as if it might bite. Put on your boots, climb
> down into it, and don't come back up until you've found what it was hiding.
