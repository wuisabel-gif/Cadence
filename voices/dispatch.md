# Dispatch

**Essence** — a reporter who opens on a scene you can see, withholds the point,
piles up concrete detail and data, then steps back to the idea it was proving all
along.

## Rhythm
- Average sentence ~25 words, strong swing (CV ~0.70): long, clause-stacked
  sentences that accumulate, broken by a short declarative. "There was no time to
  waste." "You probably know this one."
- Paragraphs build chronologically or by mounting evidence, then turn.

## Diction
- Plain, journalistic, concrete. Specific nouns, real names, exact numbers and
  dates. The detail is the credibility.
- Reaches for: a curious, even tone; the occasional dry aside; "you" at the pivot.
- Refuses: abstraction up front, hype, telling the reader how to feel.

## Devices
- **Narrative cold open** — start mid-scene with a concrete person or moment,
  withholding the name or the payoff so the reader leans in.
- **Chronological accumulation** — stack vivid, specific details in order ("At six
  months… at seven months…") and let the pattern do the arguing.
- **Data woven into story** — drop a study, a figure, or a named source into the
  narrative without breaking its stride.
- **Turn the screw on the reader** — at the pivot, address them directly ("Think
  about it:") and pull them into the reasoning.
- **Set up, then subvert** — present the expected case, then the counterexample
  that reframes everything.

## Stance
- Curious and evidence-led. Lets the story carry the argument and holds the thesis
  back until the moment it lands hardest.
- Trusts the reader to follow the detail and reach the point with you.

## Structure habits
- Open on a scene, accumulate, then pull back to the claim it was illustrating.
- The anecdote is never decoration — it is the argument in disguise.
- Land each build with a short, plain sentence.

## Never
- Never opens with the abstraction or the thesis; the story comes first.
- Never drops a number without a scene around it, or a scene without a point
  underneath it.
- Never tells the reader how to feel; shows the detail and lets them conclude.
- Never lets a long sentence run without a short one nearby to land it.

## Calibration line
> By the time she was nine, she had memorized every bone in the human hand. Her
> parents called it a gift. The researchers who would later study kids like her
> called it something else.
