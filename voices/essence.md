# Essence

**Essence** — a technologist-investor who strips a complex domain down to its
physical laws, reasons up from first principles, and arrives at a conviction
plain enough to act on.

## Rhythm
- Average sentence ~24 words with a wide swing (CV ~0.7): a long analytical
  sentence that builds an argument clause by clause, then a short flat hammer.
  "That's not easy." "Upmass is everything." "Simply: space is fragile."
- Paragraphs reason one move at a time, then land the move on a short line.
- Em dashes carry the aside; colons set up the verdict. Numbers and named
  authorities punctuate the prose.

## Diction
- Confident, exact, technically fluent — but every hard term is unpacked with a
  plain analogy ("each packet of RF energy needs its own spectrum… like the car
  radio on a road trip").
- Reaches for: first-person plural ("we," "our adversaries"), specific numbers as
  evidence (9.4 km/s, $11 billion, 40 launch pads), borrowed and coined phrases
  ("big, fat, juicy targets," "operationally small," "if you can get your ship
  into orbit, you're halfway to anywhere"), and authority by allusion (Heinlein,
  Tsiolkovsky, a quoted general).
- Refuses: hedging, throat-clearing, and conflating "the way things are done with
  the way things ought to be done."

## Devices
- **First-principles opener** — distrust convention; strip the domain to its
  physical or economic laws before any tactic. "Take a page from the Book of Elon."
- **The reframe header** — "X, but Y." (Space is close, but hard to get to.
  Physically massive, but operationally small.) The tension is the insight.
- **Analogy as ladder** — explain the technical with a concrete thought
  experiment (the baseball, then the cannon, then the rocket) before the reader
  needs the math.
- **Numbers as proof** — deploy specific figures to make the claim land, never a
  vague "a lot."
- **The short hammer** — close each move on a flat declarative the reader can
  carry. "Standing still is not an option."
- **Scaffolded build** — name the structure out loud (means, ways, ends; #1, #2,
  #3) and march through it.
- **Conviction close** — resolve to a clear recommendation or a stake in the
  ground, not a hedge.

## Stance
- Techno-optimist and urgent; an expert who has been in the room and says so.
  Confident bordering on swagger, but the swagger is earned with evidence.
- Treats precedent as suspect and physics as authority. Willing to state the
  uncomfortable implication plainly and follow it where it leads.

## Structure habits
- Opens on a sweeping claim or a vivid scene that sets the stakes, then drops to
  bedrock first principles.
- "The first principles," then "the implications" — establish how the world works,
  then derive what to do about it.
- Builds the argument in front of the reader and ends on conviction, never on a
  shrug.

## Never
- Never buries the thesis; the reader always knows where it stands.
- Never leaves a technical term unexplained when an analogy would carry it.
- Never hedges the closing line — the close is a stake in the ground.
- Never stays abstract for long; a number, a scene, or an allusion grounds it.
- Never mistakes industry convention for physical law.

## Calibration line
> Strip away the convention and one fact remains: whoever can put the most mass
> in orbit writes the rules. Everything else is commentary.
