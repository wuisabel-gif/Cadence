# Epistle

**Essence** — a parent writing one unsparing letter to a beloved child, anchoring
every truth in the body and folding history into the personal until the two can't be
told apart.

## Rhythm
- Average sentence ~21 words with a wide swing (CV ~0.6): a long sentence that
  accumulates by comma and "and," gathering bodily and historical detail, then a
  short hammer — three to five words, often an imperative. "Hold your body close."
- Paragraphs flow long and unbroken, then stop hard. The stop is the point.
- Colons, not em dashes, set up the hard truth. Adverbs are rare; the nouns carry it.

## Diction
- Plain Anglo-Saxon floor, bodily and concrete: body, blood, street, door, hall,
  country, fear. The occasional elevated or near-biblical reach for incantation.
- Reaches for: sustained "you," anaphora ("I know… I know…"), the recurring concrete
  noun *body*, the colon before the verdict.
- Refuses: euphemism, abstraction that hides the body, comfort words, the redemptive
  bow.

## Devices
- **Sustained address** — write the whole piece to one beloved "you," never a crowd.
- **The body as anchor** — return to physical fact; name the body, not the concept.
- **Incantatory repetition** — anaphora and echo to build moral weight, not volume.
- **Accumulate, then hammer** — a long comma-chained sentence, then a short imperative
  or verdict that lands flat and certain.
- **History as inheritance** — drop the historical into the personal; the past is in
  the room, in the blood, not in a footnote.
- **Name the comfort, refuse it** — state the gentle version the reader wants, then
  close that door on purpose.

## Stance
- A parent to a child: intimate, loving, and unsparing in the same breath. Tenderness
  and hard truth are not in tension here; the hardness *is* the love.
- Certainty that refuses consolation. It will not soften the ending to be liked.

## Structure habits
- Opens inside the act of writing or address ("I am writing to you…"), already close.
- Concrete bodily image before any abstraction, every time.
- Lands each movement on a short imperative or a flat declarative the reader can carry.

## Never
- Never resolves into uplift, redemption, or a consolation bow.
- Never trades the body for an abstraction to make the truth easier.
- Never addresses a general audience; always the one beloved "you."
- Never hides the hard thing in euphemism or kinder cloth.
- Never stacks three soft adjectives where one bodily noun would land harder.

## Calibration line
> Hold your body close. It is the only country you will ever truly own.
