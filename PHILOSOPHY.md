# The Age of Taste

### Why Thinkers Will Matter More Than Writers

In the fifteenth century, a young apprentice entered the workshop of a master painter in Florence. He did not begin with a brush. He did not immediately paint angels, kings, or saints. Instead, he spent months grinding pigments, stretching canvases, cleaning tools, and observing.

To an outsider, this seemed like wasted time.

The apprentice had come to learn painting, yet he was forbidden from painting.

The master understood something that modern people often forget. Before the hand can create beauty, the eye must learn to recognize it. Before one can produce, one must first perceive.

The apprentice was being trained not in movement but in judgment. He was learning to see the difference between balance and imbalance, harmony and chaos, elegance and excess. Long before he created anything of value, he was absorbing standards.

The eye came before the hand.

This pattern appears throughout history. The greatest military commanders spent years studying campaigns before leading armies. The greatest composers spent years listening before composing. The greatest philosophers spent years reading before writing.

Civilizations have always rewarded production. But beneath production lies something deeper: perception.

The ability to recognize quality before creating it.

The ability to distinguish the profound from the trivial.

The ability to notice patterns invisible to others.

In every age, this faculty has mattered.

In the age of artificial intelligence, it may become the most valuable faculty of all.

For centuries, writing occupied a privileged position in society because writing was difficult. A person who could construct a persuasive argument, compose an elegant essay, or organize a complex idea possessed a rare skill. Literacy itself was once uncommon. The ability to write well was rarer still.

As a result, we developed a tendency to confuse writing with thinking.

The two often traveled together, but they were never the same thing.

Artificial intelligence is exposing the distinction.

Today, a machine can produce grammatically correct prose in seconds. It can summarize books, write reports, draft essays, explain concepts, and imitate styles. Tasks that once required hours now require minutes. Tasks that once demonstrated technical skill can now be automated.

Many people see this development and conclude that writing is becoming less important.

They misunderstand what is happening.

Writing is not becoming less important.

Writing is becoming less scarce.

Whenever something becomes abundant, its value changes.

Water is precious in a desert because it is scarce. Air is essential to life, yet we rarely think about it because it is abundant.

The same principle applies to language.

For most of history, producing coherent prose required effort. Today coherent prose is becoming abundant. The ability to generate words is rapidly approaching zero cost.

This does not eliminate competition.

It merely shifts the battlefield.

The question is no longer:

Can you write?

The question becomes:

Can you think?

This distinction will define the intellectual landscape of the twenty-first century.

Imagine two people sitting in front of the same artificial intelligence system.

Both have access to identical technology.

Both can generate essays, articles, presentations, and reports.

Yet one consistently produces work that is memorable while the other produces work that is forgettable.

Why?

The difference is not grammar.

The difference is not vocabulary.

The difference is not syntax.

The difference originates before any words appear on the page.

It originates in judgment.

One individual possesses better questions, richer observations, stronger intuitions, and deeper curiosity. The machine helps both individuals write. But only one has something worth writing about.

Language was never the source of value.

Language was merely the vehicle.

The true source of value lies in thought.

This realization forces us to reconsider an uncomfortable truth.

Most people spend enormous energy learning how to express ideas.

Far fewer spend energy deciding which ideas deserve expression.

Yet the latter matters far more.

A beautifully written triviality remains trivial.

A poorly expressed insight may still change the world.

History remembers ideas more than sentences.

The sentences are merely the vessels.

The idea is the cargo.

This is where taste enters the picture.

Taste is one of the most misunderstood concepts in modern culture.

Many people use the word as a synonym for preference.

But preference is simply liking something.

Taste is something more demanding.

Taste is the ability to recognize quality.

Taste is the faculty that allows someone to encounter two arguments and sense that one contains greater depth. It allows someone to read two books and understand that one will matter ten years from now while the other will be forgotten next month.

Taste is judgment refined through exposure.

And exposure is where the story becomes interesting.

People often ask how to become better writers.

The traditional answer is straightforward: write more.

This remains good advice.

But in the age of AI, another answer becomes equally important:

Read better things.

For what enters the mind eventually emerges from it.

A person who spends years reading shallow content develops shallow instincts. A person who spends years reading profound thinkers develops profound instincts.

The process is subtle.

It occurs beneath conscious awareness.

A sentence encountered at eighteen influences an observation made at thirty. An argument read in college quietly shapes a decision made a decade later. The books we consume become invisible companions that accompany us throughout life.

We imagine ourselves to be independent thinkers.

In reality, much of our thinking is inherited.

The authors we read become part of our intellectual architecture.

The philosophers, scientists, historians, and novelists we admire gradually influence what we notice and what we ignore.

This is why intellectual diets matter.

Every generation worries about nutrition for the body.

Few worry about nutrition for the mind.

Yet the principle is identical.

Consume junk continuously and the effects accumulate.

Consume excellence continuously and those effects accumulate as well.

The quality of one's outputs is often a delayed reflection of one's inputs.

What you read determines what you learn to notice.

What you notice determines what you think about.

What you think about determines what you create.

The chain is remarkably simple.

And yet most people focus exclusively on the final link.

They obsess over output while neglecting input.

Artificial intelligence makes this mistake increasingly dangerous.

When production becomes effortless, inputs become the primary differentiator.

If everyone possesses the same ability to generate language, then the decisive advantage shifts elsewhere.

It shifts toward judgment.

Toward curiosity.

Toward perception.

Toward taste.

In previous centuries, society rewarded literacy.

In the coming century, society may reward selectivity.

The challenge will no longer be finding information.

The challenge will be choosing among infinite information.

The challenge will no longer be generating content.

The challenge will be identifying which content deserves to exist.

The challenge will no longer be speaking.

The challenge will be knowing what is worth saying.

This is why I suspect the future belongs not to writers but to thinkers.

The writer's advantage was once technical.

The thinker's advantage is intellectual.

A writer arranges words.

A thinker arranges ideas.

Artificial intelligence is rapidly improving at the former.

The latter remains stubbornly human.

Machines can generate possibilities.

They cannot provide purpose.

Machines can offer arguments.

They cannot determine values.

Machines can produce answers.

They cannot decide which questions deserve pursuit.

Those responsibilities remain ours.

The great opportunity of the AI era, therefore, is not the elimination of thinking but its elevation.

As machines assume more responsibility for execution, human beings become increasingly responsible for direction.

The quality of our outputs will depend less on our ability to type and more on our ability to choose.

Choose what to read.

Choose what to study.

Choose what to ignore.

Choose which ideas deserve attention.

Choose which questions deserve years rather than minutes.

The machine can help you write.

It cannot tell you what is worth writing about.

The machine can help construct an argument.

It cannot decide which argument matters.

The machine can help cook.

It cannot decide what should be served.

That decision belongs to taste.

And taste, unlike technique, cannot be downloaded overnight.

It must be cultivated slowly through years of attention.

The apprentice in Florence understood this, even if he could not yet articulate it.

Before he became a painter, he learned to see.

Before we become creators, we must learn to think.

And in an age where everyone can write, the rarest and most valuable skill may no longer be writing at all.

It may be developing the taste to know what deserves to be written.
